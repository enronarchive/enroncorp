Our History

 

In 1998, Enron Corp. formed a new venture to pursue opportunities in the global water industry.


In October 1998, Azurix acquired 100 percent of Wessex Water Plc, regarded by the industry regulator as the most efficiently operated water and waste water utility in England and Wales.

In December 1998, Azurix acquired Enron's 32 percent interest in Obras Sanitarias de Mendoza S.A. (OSM), a company responsible for water distribution and treatment in the Mendoza province of Argentina.

In March 1999, Azurix acquired a 49.9 percent interest in the water and wastewater concession for the municipalities of Cancun and Isla Mujeres in the state of Quintana Roo on Mexico's Yucatan Peninsula.

Azurix North America acquired Philip Utilities Management Corporation (PUMC) in May 1999. This company's primary businesses are consistent with the opportunities Azurix is pursuing worldwide, including: operations, management and engineering; residuals management; underground infrastructure; and ownership of water and wastewater assets.

On June 30, 1999, Azurix Buenos Aires S.A. signed a 30-year contract with the Province of Buenos Aires to operate the water and wastewater services concessions in this fast growing area. Azurix Buenos Aires S.A. anticipates expanding operations to surrounding municipalities and offering industrial customers cost-effective alternatives.

Azurix entered into an agreement to acquire a 49 percent of Industrias del Aqua, S.A. de C.V. (IASA), a water and wastewater service company based in Monterrey, Mexico in May 1999. Through a subsidiary, IASA provides metering, billing, collections, operations and maintenance services.

In September 1999, Azurix acquired 100 percent of the business interests of AMX Acqua Management Inc, a Brazilian company that provides water resource development and management services, including the exploration and storage of water.

Azurix signed an agreement to acquire 100 percent of Lurgi Bamag GmbH, a process engineering company specializing in the water sector in September 1999. Lurgi Bamag serves a strong base of blue chip industrial customers in refining, pulp & paper, petrochemicals and iron & steel who require cost-efficient, high quality water and wastewater treatment.

In September 1999, Azurix announced its participation in the construction and operation of two wastewater treatment plants in Leon and Torreon, Mexico through a joint venture with FYPASA Construcciones S.A. de C.V.

And in October, Azurix acquired 100 percent of J & J Baker, Inc. and its affiliate Nutri-Cycle, Inc., providers of wastewater bio-solids management and land applications services in Florida.

In October 1999, Azurix launched an innovative aquifer development and storage project by purchasing 13,600 acres of strategically located land in Madera County, California. It is hoped that the aquifer on the property will enable customers to "bank" water during wet years to be made available during drier periods. The Madera Water Bank project is under development.

In December 1999, Azurix acquired 100% of privately held Terratec Environmental Ltd., a municipal biosolids recycling company in Ontario. Terratec services 21 wastewater treatment plants and storage facilities in the Halton Region of Ontario between Hamilton and Toronto, including the facilities for the city of Toronto.

In May 2000, Azurix North America and the City of Wildwood, New Jersey announced a 20-year, $71 million operation and maintenance contract for water services. Azurix North America now operates the city's water system, which has 12,000 connections.

And in July, Azurix acquired Baker Hughes Industrial Services, Inc. (BHIS), an industrial water and wastewater operations and engineering support company primarily serving customers in the refinery and petrochemical market. BHIS is now named Azurix Industrial Operations Corp. and is part of Azurix Industrials Corp., which provides outsourcing services to industrial customers.

Today, Azurix continues to develop and operate water and wastewater assets and bring innovative risk management and financial strategies to the water market.

Making the vision a reality . . .

The global water market faces challenges today that cannot be overcome without innovative solutions.

  • Providing safe, clean drinking water is becoming ever more complex.
  • Rapid population growth combined with extensive commercial and industrial development has strained water and wastewater infrastructure worldwide.
  • In the United States, many municipal water systems are in need of repair or replacement.
  • In Europe, and other parts of the world, certain water system infrastructures are several hundred years old. Many of these assets are operating under the strain of usage levels far beyond their original capacity.
  • The United States Environmental Protection Agency estimates that over half of the approximately $275 billion of capital that will be spent in the United States during the next twenty years will be spent on repairing, replacing and developing the nation's underground water and wastewater infrastructure.
