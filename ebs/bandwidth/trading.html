Today’s market for bandwidth is inflexible, inefficient, and archaic. Currently, a single bandwidth transaction requires months to negotiate, close, and implement. Non-standard, one-off service level agreements and legal contracts mean operations and legal teams further delay the process. The final result is usually a difficult, rigid contract lasting several years. EBS’s objective is to allow similar transactions to be closed in seconds and implemented immediately, initially in monthly terms.

Pooling Points

Pooling points connect telecommunications carriers, allowing data to move quickly from one network to another. As pooling points are developed, bandwidth trading will become more efficient and more common. A pooling point is defined as an interconnection point-of-presence at a selected physical location through which:

  1. Connections between buyer and seller are reserved,
  2. Connections are established between buyers and sellers at specified capacity rates, and
  3. The QoS of the connection is monitored.
The physical interconnectivity of all market participants in the form of pooling points facilitates the trading of standardized bandwidth units. Pooling points should be implemented in pairs at the two ends of city-pair segments that represent the most liquid telecommunications paths.
Enron Broadband Services established the first two pooling points in 1999 in New York and Los Angeles. Within the next 2-3 years, EBS expects that up to 100 pooling points will be built in North America, with another 100 points internationally.

Commoditization

As the industry accepts the commoditization of bandwidth, a more efficient and flexible market will develop. Commodity producers will provide value-added services that capture a much larger margin than the commodity itself. If an ISP needs a two-hour block of bandwidth tomorrow—how much do you think the ISP is willing to pay for this service? The only way to provide such a high- value service is to have an efficient commodity market. Efficient markets spark innovation, benefiting producers (who capture additional margin by differentiating product) and consumers (who obtain a precise solution to a business need).

Enron Broadband Services along with industry participants has developed a standardized terms and conditions contract that allows for efficient commodity trading of bandwidth. A standard bandwidth contract that pools existing contracts or unused capacity that can be traded in a marketplace is one of the first steps in establishing a true commodity market for bandwidth.

Whether or not we call it a commodity, bandwidth is just that. Once network speed and quality of service are constant, the bandwidth capacity that transports a specific packet is indistinguishable from any other bandwidth capacity. It’s also perishable, just like seats on an airplane. Once the cabin door closes, all the empty seats on the plane are perished and spoiled. If those seats are filled at the last minute, the revenue received is in fact pure opportunity profit. A commodity market, with real-time provisioning and quality certification, allows this sort of last minute seat-filling.
