The Art of Innovation

It would be hard to find an architect today more famous for his monumental structures or more celebrated for his mastery of construction than Frank Gehry. But perhaps his greatest achievement (and the true source of our admiration) is his deconstruction of convention, for the greatest building begins with the demolition of obsolete ideas.

This creative destruction is innovation as defined by Gary Hamel, business consultant, professor, and author. But Hamel hardly sanctions the wholesale destruction of the old to make room for the new. Instead, he counsels students and clients alike to scrutinize the rules that govern almost anything -- an industry, an artform, our lives. Examine them from all sides and perspectives, and finally, look underneath them. Propping up some of these rules you'll find deep-seated principles and underlying truths. Underneath others, you'll simply find more rules. "One of the most important lenses in any company is being able to . . . distinguish orthodoxy from deep principle," he writes in Leading the Revolution. The future is created by "heretics," says Hamel -- the Gandhis and Picassos and Gehrys of the world who challenge every established law with one simple question: "why?"

It's a question so central to Enron that they've made it their mantra: "ask why." Enron has a history of questioning the status quo, not only changing the way they do business, but changing the way business is done. Their mission is the search for these underlying principles, for they believe that beyond the rules of conventional thinking lies a better way, waiting to be discovered.

This discovery is what Gehry calls "the moment of truth." It's the intersection between what must be and what could be, as when an architect brings artistry to a building's necessary functionality. No one would confuse Enron's business savvy with artistic sensibilities, but there is a real creativity behind their work. While the artist fuses many sources and influences into one work (one look at the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao reveals influences as diverse as a fish, Fritz Lang's Metropolis, and Brancusi's studio), Enron turns this creative process on its head, taking one model and expanding it into many applications.

Enron did just this when they revolutionized the gas industry and went on to apply their ground-breaking new model to markets as diverse as electricity, paper, and bandwidth capacity. With each adaptation, Enron made the market more efficient and, in some cases, created markets where none had existed before. With each new opportunity Enron encounters, their ubiquitous "why" is met most often with "why not?"

While their approaches to innovation differ greatly, Enron and Frank Gehry both face the same question at the end of the workday: how to create energy, movement, flow? And whether their medium requires concrete and steel or gas and electricity, the process remains the same: begin with the underlying truth. Let your plans flow from there.
