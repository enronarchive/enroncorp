Enron Cost of Credit (ECC)

The Enron Cost of Credit (ECC) is a real-time "price" of credit for a given company. Unlike other qualitative credit ratings that are currently available, the ECC is quantitative and market-driven. The ECC does not necessarily replace other credit ratings on the market, but rather supplements those ratings with a more sensitive, market-driven measure of credit risk. ECC figures differentiate relative credit quality to 1/100th of a percent and are updated daily. Enron Credit currently has ECC listings available for over 10,000 companies worldwide.
 
The ECC can be easily incorporated into your existing credit analysis processes. The ECC can assist you in :
    
Comparing credit risks of potential customers, suppliers and business partners
     Pricing new deals and business opportunities to account for credit risk
     Setting dynamic credit limits
     Hedging when necessary


Enron calculates the ECC for each company by taking the estimated probability of bankruptcy and multiplying this by the expected percentage of a claim that a senior ranking unsecured creditor would lose in the event of the company's bankruptcy.

In its simplest form, the ECC can be expressed by the following formula:

ECC = Probability of Bankruptcy * (1- Recovery Rate)

This formula is then adjusted to account for credit differentials such as event discounts and liquidity factors.

You can think of the ECC as a premium for credit risk. It represents the premium over your own cost of funding that you may charge for producing credit to a particular company. The ECC is expressed as a simple interest rate. For instance, it costs more to extend credit to a company with an ECC of 2% than to a company with an ECC of 1.5%. On our website, the ECC is listed for several time periods. You can select the ECC for the time period that best matches your credit exposure.

Example: You have a one-year contract with company X and you have estimated that under this contract you have credit exposure of US$2 million. You see that the one-year ECC for this company is 1.5%. Therefore extending credit to this company for one year is costing your company US$30,000 (i.e. US$2,000,000 * 1.5%).
