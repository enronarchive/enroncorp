What is Enron Credit?

Back to top

Based in London, England, Enron Credit allows businesses to evaluate, monitor and mitigate credit exposure through a variety of tools. Enron Credit is creating a more efficient credit market by helping to increase liquidity through standardization and transparency.

An evolutionary idea, Enron Credit was originally developed through Enron Corp. to help its own commercial traders manage today's accelerated pace of business transactions. Credit was a logical market for Enron to grow into because of their unparalleled core competency in risk management and recent success with the development of EnronOnline™, a commodities platform that completed more than 550,000 transactions with 1120 products in 13 currencies and surpassed $330 billion of notional value in 2000. The need for real time credit prices has accelerated because of the Internet. Enron Credit is now providing its advanced, proven credit tools and bankruptcy swap products to traders and companies at large. Enron Credit is perfectly poised to lead the way to a more efficient credit market.

Enron Credit understands the technology and financial concerns common in the credit market. They are committed to the following principles: credit has a price, credit exposures can be tracked in real time and credit exposures can be quickly hedged.

What is the Enron Cost of Credit™?

Back to top

The Enron Cost of Credit (ECC) is a real-time "price" of credit for a given company calculated using Enron Credit's proprietary model. Unlike other qualitative credit ratings that are currently available, the ECC is quantitative and market-driven. Enron Credit provides this free measurement of credit risk for more than 10,000 companies on our website. The Enron Cost of Credit is a sophisticated, real-time analysis of dozens of credit risk indicators that help companies estimate their expected loss on extending credit to a given counterparty.

How often is the ECC updated?

Back to top

The ECC is updated in real-time as new information becomes available. The latest information is always accessible on enroncredit.com. Users also have the ability to receive daily or weekly data feeds that deliver customized ECC data to the users desktop.

How does this differ from a credit rating index?

Back to top

The Enron Cost of Credit is as a supplement to traditional credit ratings. It is market-driven and updated in real-time, and is therefore, more reflective of current market conditions than a traditional credit rating. This allows users to become aware of changing credit risks earlier and address them before problems arise.

What is credit exposure?

Back to top

Whenever a company sells merchandise to another company without cash up front, there is a certain probability that the customer will not pay. That risk is commonly referred to as credit exposure. There are broader implications of the term, as well - companies are exposed in similar ways to suppliers, outsourcing providers, and even their customers' customers.

Does this product compete with insurance companies and insurance products?

Back to top

Credit derivatives differ from insurance products in two important ways: they are liquid, and they are quick to implement. Liquidity is important -- it means that a company can buy and sell coverage to suit its current situation. Speed to implement is also key in today's environment, as many companies don't want to take the time to go through the process review and documentation required to open an insurance agreement.

What benefit does a Digital Bankruptcy Swap (DBS) offer the parties involved?

Back to top

A DBS gives companies the ability to make their credit portfolios more liquid giving them the ability to buy and sell coverage to suit their current situation. If risk is concentrated in a particular company, industry, or geographic region, a DBS allows a company to buy and sell protection, mitigating risk.

How does a credit derivative, e.g. a Credit Default Swap (CDS), differ from a Digital Bankruptcy Swap (DBS)?

Back to top

While a CDS has up to seven triggering events (some of which are lacking in clarity), the Digital Bankruptcy Swap has one: bankruptcy. DBS's are best suited for the corporate market, because businesses eventually pay their debts to primary suppliers. In addition, because it is a publicly observable event, it is a very clear trigger.
