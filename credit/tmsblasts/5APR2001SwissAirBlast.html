Unhappy Landings

Source: Enron Credit Blasts
Issue: 05 April 2001
Author: Enron Credit - VJB

At the beginning of this week, SAirGroup, the parent company of Swissair, Crossair and Balair, reported a net loss of CHF 2.9 billion for 2000 – the worst results in its 70-year history. Results from the group’s airline-related divisions including cargo services and logistics were encouraging, but could not compensate for the negative influences on the airline side.

Although some analysts had predicted a less than favourable year, the loss was greater than expected considering a net profit of CHF 273 million in 1999. Losses were reportedly incurred by investments in French, Belgian and German airlines as well as their flagship Swissair, and compounded by the hike in jet fuel prices – last year the price of Swissair’s fuel jumped 56 per cent (CHF 270 million).

On Monday 2nd April 2001, SAirGroup stock, which has fallen 31 percent this year, was suspended on the Swiss Exchange.hThe company’s equity has dropped from CHF 4.2 billion to CHF 1.2 billion and its debt equity ratio has risen from 1.01 to 4.68 – primarily because of the cost of writing down airline investments. Additionally, Moody’s Investors Service downgraded SAirGroup’s issuer rating to Baa3 after placing its previous rating of A3 on negative watch on 26th January 2001. The company’s short term rating was also downgraded to P-3. The downturn in SAirGroup’s perceived credit quality illustrates the importance of proactive credit management.

Any company may reduce its credit risk by carefully evaluating and monitoring individual counterparty or entire portfolio risk and then hedging against that exposure. Companies dealing with entities such as SAirGroup, can compare their relative creditworthiness to other companies within the airline sector by examining the Enron Cost of Credit (ECC). Where necessary any associated cost of credit can then be incorporate into deal structures. The ECC represents Enron Credit’s opinion, prepared in accordance with its proprietary model, as to the cost of extending trade credit to counterparties. The ECC can be used to evaluate deals, enabling companies to more easily determine when to hedge their risks with tools such as Digital Bankruptcy Swaps (DBS) or Credit Default Swaps (CDS).
