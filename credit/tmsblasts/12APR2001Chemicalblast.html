Unpredictable Chemical Reactions

Source: Enron Credit Blasts
Issue: 12 April 2001
Author: Enron Credit -BS

Like most companies, chemical companies tend to have significant exposure to particular commodities. As commodity prices rise, companies often have a difficult time passing a corresponding price increase on to their customers. As a result, cash flows become critical and credit quality can easily deteriorate.

In recent months, the chemical industry has been hit with high raw material costs and low demand. Despite the negative economic conditions, some analysts believe that as natural gas and other commodity prices stabilize, most chemical companies will be able to weather the storm. Because of this, the chemical sector is actually showing signs of improvement in the credit market. Credit spreads in the chemical industry have tightened recently, as reflected in the Enron Cost of Credit (ECC).

However, the chemical indices only tell part of the story. Although the overall chemical industry is showing signs of improvement, two chemical companies filed for bankruptcy in the past week. The two companies, W.R. Grace and Borden Chemicals and Plastics, filed for bankruptcy for vastly different reasons – demonstrating that despite improving credit market conditions, companies are still exposed to many types of credit risk.

W.R. Grace filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy on April 2 citing the primary cause as asbestos-related litigation. The company has received over 325,000 asbestos-related legal claims to date and has spent over $1.9 billion managing and resolving those claims. Considering that the company’s annual sales in 2000 were approximately $1.6 billion, it is easy to see how costly litigation could take a toll on the company’s profitability. At the time of the bankruptcy filing, Grace reported $2.5 billion in assets and $2.57 billion in debts. W. R. Grace joins a number of other companies, including Owens Corning G-I Holdings Inc. and Armstrong World Industries Inc., who have filed for bankruptcy protection within the past year due to asbestos-related litigation.

In comparison, high natural gas prices and low PVC demand had a detrimental impact on Borden Chemicals and Plastics. As natural gas prices continued to increase while demand and prices in resins – their core business – continued to decline, Borden Chemical and Plastics’ financial situation worsened. On April 3, the company was forced to file Chapter 11 bankruptcy and is seeking the bankruptcy court’s approval of a $100 million debtor-in-possession credit facility. Between October 2000 and February 2001, credit spreads for Borden Chemicals and Plastics widened by over 1800 basis points.

A recent Accenture survey commissioned by Enron Credit shows that 31% of credit managers have an annual credit review process and 61% review their situation “as needed.” However, the above examples demonstrate how important it is to regularly monitor the quality of your credit risk portfolio. Even if the overall market is improving, there is always a chance that a specific company in a given sector might go bankrupt. Frequent monitoring is essential so you are not caught off-guard when a company takes a sudden downturn.

The Enron Cost of Credit (ECC) is a free tool credit managers can use to assess the quality of their credit portfolio. The ECC is a market-driven measure of risk that can be used to track changes in a company’s credit quality over time. Credit managers can also mitigate risk by purchasing protection against specific companies in their credit portfolio.

By closely monitoring your credit portfolio and hedging exposures as needed, you can reduce the chance your company will experience a sudden, unexpected loss due to the bankruptcy of a counterparty.
